Contest is the UK government's counter-terrorism strategy, aimed at reducing the risk of terrorism and violent extremism. there are four Ps to CONTEST;
Prevent,
Pursue,
Protect,
and Prepare

Prevent training is designed to help professionals identify and support individuals at risk of radicalisation. The government provides free online prevent training. Find out more

Of all the Prevent referrals in 2023/2024: 36% were related to individuals with a vulnerability present but no ideology or CT risk
19% to Extreme Right-Wing
13% regarding Islamist radicalisation
2% to concerns regarding school massacres,
1% of concerns were incel-related.

Where age was known, individuals aged 11 to 15 accounted for the largest proportion (40%) referrals, with individuals aged 16 to 17 accounting for 13%, followed by individuals aged 31 to 40 (10%).

Extremism has been a growing threat for many years. The attacks in Israel on 7 October 2023 highlighted how serious and widespread extremist ideologies have become, showing the need for stronger action.
The government's new definition of extremism updates the 2011 Prevent Strategy. It reflects how extremist movements have changed and the social harms they create. The aim is clear: to protect citizens, uphold democracy, and keep the country safe.
Most extremist activity isn't illegal, but it can still be harmful. In the UK, Islamist and Neo-Nazi groups are working legally yet openly encourage radicalisation and try to undermine democracy. Their actions erode rights, freedoms, and social cohesion — and can lead to terrorism.
The Independent Review of Prevent stressed the need to challenge extremist ideologies, not just violent ones, but also those that spread hate, limit free speech, or weaken democratic institutions.
Alongside this definition, the government has set out engagement principles to make sure extremist groups aren't given platforms, funding, or legitimacy. This is part of a wider effort that also includes updated Prevent and CONTEST strategies, the Defending Democracy Taskforce, and the Integrated Review.
This new approach is narrower, clearer, and more precise about extremist behaviours and harms. It builds on the 2021 Operating with Impunity report by Dame Sara Khan and Sir Mark Rowley, showing that freedom of expression can be protected while tackling dangerous extremist activity. Importantly, this definition is not aimed at people with conservative views — it is focused solely on harmful extremist ideologies.

If a referral progresses and it is assessed that there is a genuine risk of radicalisation, the case is considered by a multi-agency 'Channel panel' of professionals who collectively assess the case and decide on a tailored package of support that can be offered to the person.

The Channel panel is chaired by the local authority and can include a variety of partners such as the police, children's services, social services, education professionals and mental health care professionals.

What kind of support is offered via Channel?
If a Channel intervention is required, a tailored support package will be offered. This could include mentoring, theological guidance, education, and careers assistance to those assessed as being at risk of radicalisation and requiring support. This is known as Channel in England and Wales, and Prevent Multi-Agency Panels (PMAP) in Scotland.

Channel is voluntary and people who are referred to Prevent must give consent (via a parent or guardian if they are underage) before they can be given support.

If a person does not engage with Channel or decides not to continue with the process for any reason, alternative forms of support may be available from the local authority or other providers. Any risks are then carefully managed by the police.
