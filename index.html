What is the prevent strategy?

Prevent is one of the four parts of the UK's CONTEST counter-terrorism strategy. It is about stopping people from being drawn into extremist or terrorist ideas before it's too late. Prevent focuses on early intervention, protecting those who may be vulnerable and giving them the support they need.

Since 2015, Prevent has been a legal duty for many organisations, including schools, colleges, universities, the NHS, local councils, probation services, and the police. This means staff in these places must be trained to spot the signs of radicalisation and know how to respond.

The aim isn't to punish people, it's to safeguard them. Just like protecting children from neglect or abuse, Prevent protects people from dangerous ideologies that could harm them or others.

Signs of radicalization

Accessing extreme material

This may include seeking out content that promotes extremist views, often through online platforms.

Justifying violence

This may include making excuses for violent actions or promoting the idea that violence is a justified response to certain situations.

Sudden style change

This may include a drastic shift in clothing choices, often to fit in with a new peer group or ideology and may include extreme symbols.

Not engaging with others

This may include avoiding socialising, not participating in group activities, or having no interest in conversations.

Peer pressure

This may include feeling pressured to conform to the behaviors, beliefs, or values of a peer group, often leading to risky choices.

Using extremist symbols

This may include displaying or wearing symbols, logos, or clothing associated with extremist groups or ideologies.

Types of extremism

Far right / nationalist

This may include promoting exclusionary or supremacist ideologies, often targeting specific racial, ethnic, or religious groups.

Islamist extremist

While referrals are going down for islamist extremists they are still a concern and numbers are high in prisons and among young men.

Conflicted concerns

One of the largest categories of referrals, these are often linked to mental health issues or personal grievances rather than a specific ideology.

School massacre

While less common school massacres are still a concern and often linked to other forms of extremism such as the far right or incel ideology.

Incel

Although still a small number of referrals these are increasing and often linked to misogynistic beliefs and a sense of grievance.

Reporting concerns

If you're ever worried that someone you know might be at risk of being drawn into extremist or harmful ideas, it's important to speak up. Reporting doesn't mean getting someone in trouble â€” it's about keeping them safe.

Teachers, doctors, social workers, and even the police can help by passing concerns to the local Prevent team. They will look at the situation carefully and, if needed, offer support through the Channel programme (a voluntary scheme that gives help with education, mental health, mentoring, or other needs). Remember, sharing a concern could be the first step in protecting a friend or family member from being exploited.
